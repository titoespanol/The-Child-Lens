Brand System v1.0

Child-first
Design System

A design system built to serve children first. Every color, radius, and word is calibrated for little hands, growing minds, and the caregivers who guide them.

theChildLens is a venture studio and agency specializing in pediatric digital health. We design, build, and invest in products that put children's needs at the center of every decision—from pixel to protocol.

Clarity

Every screen answers one question. Reduce cognitive load. If a child can’t parse it in 3 seconds, simplify.

Calm

Health topics trigger anxiety. Our interfaces breathe. Generous space, soft motion, warm tones. No urgency theater.

Safety

Privacy by default. No dark patterns. Parent gates for purchases. Data minimization. Children are never the product.

Agency

Children are participants, not passengers. Age-appropriate controls. Undoable actions. Celebrate exploration over perfection.

Evidence

Every health claim has a source. Disclaimers are clear, not buried. We design with pediatricians, not around them.

Philosophy

Learn More. Scroll Less.

Every screen, every flow, every component in this system is designed around one idea: Less screen. More sense. Children’s brains are still developing. Engagement-first design exploits this. We choose a different path: we measure comprehension, not consumption. We celebrate stopping, not streaking. We build products that children outgrow — and that’s the point.

The Research

Delgado et al. [3] found that reading on screens yields ~13–15% lower comprehension than paper. Clinton [4] and Støle et al. [5] confirmed this across age groups. The gap comes from passive consumption. Our system closes it by building active recall, summarization, and stopping points into every flow.

Core Principles

Clarity over Consumption

One idea per screen. Plain language. No noise. Every word earns its place.

Comprehend. Apply. Stop.

The Learn Better Loop. Active recall, real-world transfer, then a celebrated ending. Every session, every time.

Quality Minutes, Not Screen Minutes

A 3-minute session with comprehension is worth more than 30 minutes of scrolling. We track understanding, not usage.

Designed to Finish, Not to Binge

Sessions end with celebration, not cliffhangers. “Done for today” is success, not failure. Finite experiences teach children that “enough” is good.

The Five Rules

  1. No infinite scroll — Finite experiences teach children that “enough” is good.
  2. Auto-advance OFF by default — Children should control their pace; auto-advance pressures fast readers and loses slow ones.
  3. No variable rewards — Random rewards create dopaminergic loops. Consistent, predictable feedback respects developing brains.
  4. Stopping points mandatory — Every flow must have a clear, celebrated ending. “Done for today” is success, not failure.
  5. Measure quality > time — We track comprehension, not minutes. A child who understands in 3 minutes did better than one scrolling for 30.

Philosophy

What It Means to Design for Children

Pediatric digital products serve a dual audience: the child who interacts, and the caregiver who decides, supervises, and worries. Great child-first design holds both in mind at every layer.

The Dual-Audience Challenge

A child taps, swipes, and explores. A caregiver reads, evaluates, and decides. These two users have radically different needs:

  • Children need large targets, immediate feedback, forgiving interactions, playful cues, and emotional safety.
  • Caregivers need credibility signals, transparent data practices, evidence-based content, and control over what their child sees and does.
  • The interface must serve both without forcing either to work around the other. Use audience-specific views, parent gates, and progressive disclosure.

Development by Age Band

0–2 years

Sensory Explorers

  • No reading ability; rely on images, sounds, and haptics
  • Gross motor only: full-hand taps, no precision
  • Attention span: 30–90 seconds per activity
  • High repetition tolerance; love cause-and-effect loops
  • Primary user is the caregiver; child interacts passively
  • Touch targets: 64px+ minimum
3–5 years

Pattern Seekers

  • Pre-literacy: icons, shapes, colors for navigation
  • Improving fine motor: can tap, drag short distances
  • Attention: 2–5 minutes with engagement
  • Beginning to recognize letters and numbers
  • Frustrated by unexpected changes; need undo
  • Positive reinforcement > correction
6–8 years

Emerging Readers

  • Early literacy: short sentences, simple vocabulary
  • Can use swipe, scroll, drag-and-drop
  • Attention: 5–10 minutes with scaffolding
  • Developing memory; can follow 2–3 step flows
  • Social awareness emerging; avoid shame-based copy
  • Touch targets: 48px+ minimum
9–12 years

Independent Navigators

  • Fluent readers; can process paragraph-level content
  • Full gesture vocabulary; beginning keyboard use
  • Attention: 10–20 minutes with interest
  • Can handle branching flows and settings
  • Privacy awareness growing; want their own space
  • Standard touch targets work (44px)
Teen

Autonomous Users

  • Adult literacy level; complex UI is fine
  • Full device fluency; multi-app context switching
  • Attention varies by motivation; expect speed
  • Strong opinions about aesthetics and autonomy
  • Privacy is non-negotiable; demand transparency
  • Reject anything that feels "childish"
Ethics

Safety, Trust & Ethics

Pediatric digital health sits at the intersection of vulnerability and trust. These guidelines ensure our products protect children while empowering caregivers.

Dark Patterns We Reject

No infinite scroll. No engagement streaks. No notification spam. No loot boxes or random rewards. No social comparison. No data harvesting for ad targeting. No guilt-based retention ("Your plant is dying!"). These patterns exploit developing brains and are incompatible with our mission.

Reassurance by Design

Parents using health apps are often anxious. Our tone must be warm, specific, and non-alarmist.

Don't

"Warning: Your child's weight is below normal. Consult a doctor immediately."

Do

"Your child's weight is tracking a bit lower than average. This is common and often normal. Here's what you can discuss at your next check-up."

Privacy & Data Minimization

  • Collect only what is necessary for the feature to function. Ask: "Would this feature work without this data point?"
  • Caregiver consent is required before any data collection. Use clear, jargon-free consent flows.
  • Children’s data is never sold, shared with advertisers, or used for profiling.
  • Implement data retention limits. Delete data that is no longer needed.
  • Age-gate sensitive features. Use parent gates (simple math problem, hold-to-unlock) for settings, purchases, and data export.

Parent Gates

Areas that require caregiver verification before a child can access them:

  • Purchases and subscriptions: Any flow involving payment.
  • Account settings: Email, password, profile changes.
  • Data export or sharing: Sending reports to third parties.
  • External links: Any navigation outside the app.
  • Advanced configuration: Notification settings, feature toggles.

Gate Mechanism

Use a simple challenge that's easy for adults but hard for young children: "Hold this button for 3 seconds," a small math problem (12 + 7), or swiping a specific pattern. Avoid CAPTCHAs.

Disclaimer Pattern

Health disclaimers are mandatory but shouldn't create panic. Guidelines:

  • Place disclaimers near the content they reference, not buried in footers.
  • Use plain language: "This is general info, not medical advice" over "Consult a licensed healthcare professional."
  • Keep the tone warm: "Always check with your pediatrician" feels caring. "We are not liable" feels cold.
  • Visually distinguish disclaimers (subtle background, smaller text) without hiding them.
  • Never use disclaimers to excuse poor content quality.
Ethics

Engagement Guardrails

Children’s apps face a fundamental tension: engagement drives business metrics, but unchecked engagement harms children. These guardrails define the line between healthy interaction and exploitative design. Every feature must pass this filter before shipping.

Finite Sessions

Every flow has a clear end. No infinite scroll, no auto-play into the next episode, no “just one more” prompts. Sessions end with celebration, not cliffhangers.

Stopping Points

Every flow must include at least one visible stopping point. The “Done for today” CTA is always more prominent than “Continue.” Calm learning. Clear stopping points.

No Variable Rewards

Slot-machine mechanics (random loot, surprise rewards, mystery boxes) exploit dopamine pathways in developing brains. All rewards are predictable and earned through comprehension, not time spent.

No Streak Mechanics

Streaks create anxiety and guilt. A child who skips a day should feel welcomed back, not punished. Progress is cumulative, never fragile.

No Social Pressure

No leaderboards, no “your friends are playing,” no competitive rankings. Learning is personal. Comparison is harmful.

Notification Ethics

Notifications must be educational, not persuasive. Never “You haven’t visited in 3 days!” Always “New article on sleep hygiene available.” Maximum 1 notification per day, opt-in only [6].

Do

Celebrate session completion: “Great job today! See you next time.”

Do

Make the default state “off” for auto-advance, notifications, and continuous play

Don’t

“Your plant is dying!” — guilt-based retention is manipulation

Don’t

“You’ll lose your 7-day streak!” — streaks exploit loss aversion

Content System

Reading Level & Clarity Toolkit

Health literacy determines outcomes. Most U.S. adults read at a 7th–8th grade level. Most health content is written at 10th+. We bridge the gap.

Rules

  1. Target SMOG grade 5.0–6.0 for caregiver content; 3.0–5.0 for child-facing content. Use SMOG as the primary readability formula, not Flesch-Kincaid alone. SMOG is validated against 100% comprehension and is recommended by the NIH for healthcare materials. Flesch-Kincaid underestimates difficulty by approximately 2.75 grade levels on health content.
  2. Max 15 words per sentence. Aim for one idea per sentence. Based on the AHRQ Health Literacy Universal Precautions Toolkit, 3rd Edition.
  3. One idea per screen. One idea per paragraph. Follows W3C COGA guidelines for making content usable for people with cognitive and learning disabilities.
  4. Active voice always. "Your doctor reviewed the results" — not "Results were reviewed by your doctor." Active voice reduces reading time and improves comprehension by 13–15%.
  5. Replace medical jargon with plain-language equivalents. See the table below. Sourced from the AHRQ toolkit and CDC Everyday Words for Public Health.
  6. Define acronyms at first use, then avoid repeating them. If an acronym will appear only once, spell it out and skip the abbreviation entirely.
  7. Front-load the key message. The most important information goes in the first sentence. Users read 80% of the first sentence but only 20% of the third.
  8. Behavioral recommendations must be specific and actionable. "Give 5 ml every 6 hours" — not "Take medicine regularly." Per CDC Clear Communication Index criteria.

"Medical Accuracy + Calm" — The Reassurance Structure

Accurate clinical information delivered without alarmism. Use the 3-part structure: Normalize, Educate, Empower. Derived from pediatric anxiety research and mHealth communication studies.

Example ❶: Fever

❶ Normalize "Fever is the body's defense system working. Most fevers in children are caused by common viruses and resolve on their own within 2–3 days."
❷ Educate "A fever under 104°F is not dangerous by itself. The number on the thermometer matters less than how your child is acting. A playful child with 102°F is doing fine."
❸ Empower "Here's what you can do right now: offer fluids, dress them lightly, and give fever medicine if they seem uncomfortable. Check temperature again in 30 minutes."

Example ❷: Child Anxiety

❶ Normalize "About 1 in 8 children experience anxiety. It's one of the most common childhood mental health concerns, and it responds well to support."
❷ Educate "Anxiety isn't a character flaw or a parenting failure. It's the brain's alarm system being a bit too sensitive. With the right tools, children can learn to manage it."
❸ Empower "Start with one small step: practice belly breathing together for 2 minutes at bedtime. If symptoms continue for more than 2 weeks, talk to your pediatrician about next options."

Learn Better Loop

A three-step cycle that turns passive screen time into active learning. Comprehend. Apply. Stop. Every educational session in our products follows this structure.

The Science Behind the Loop

Delgado et al. [3] found that reading on screens yields ~13–15% lower comprehension than paper. Clinton [4] and Støle et al. [5] confirmed this across age groups. The gap isn’t about screens being “bad” — it’s about passive consumption. Active recall structures (testing, summarizing, applying) close this gap. The Learn Better Loop builds these structures into every session. Quality minutes, not screen minutes.

1

Comprehend

Cognitive goal: Encode the key idea. Read or watch a short unit, then answer a comprehension check. The testing effect strengthens memory more than re-reading [3][4].

→
2

Apply

Cognitive goal: Transfer knowledge. One-sentence recap (summarization forces synthesis) or a 60-second real-world action. Screen to life.

→
3

Stop

Cognitive goal: Consolidate. Celebration. “Done for today ” is the primary CTA. No guilt, no cliffhangers, no “just one more.”

Screen Reading Mitigation Kit

7 evidence-based techniques to improve comprehension on screens. Each technique has a research basis and a practical UI recommendation.

1. Segmenting

Chunk content into small units. Chunking reduces cognitive load by ~40% in developing readers. One idea per screen.

2. Signaling

Bold key terms, use headings, highlight core ideas. Children miss 3× more information without visual cues directing attention.

3. Reduce Distractions

Focus mode removes peripheral stimuli competing for limited working memory. No sidebar ads, no unrelated recommendations, no animation.

4. Comprehension Checks

The testing effect: retrieving information from memory strengthens the memory trace more than re-reading. 1 question per content unit.

5. Recap

Summarization forces synthesis — identifying the main idea, discarding irrelevant detail, restating in own words. The highest-order thinking skill.

6. Apply

Transfer from reading to action closes the knowing-doing gap. A 60-second real-world activity solidifies learning more than 10 minutes of passive scrolling.

7. Clear Ending

Finite closure reduces anxiety and builds healthy digital habits [1][2]. “Done for today” is success, not failure. Designed to finish, not to binge.

Reassurance Patterns

Five reusable content components for health communication. Each pattern includes description, usage context, and ready-to-ship copy for both audiences.

Pattern 01

Reassurance Block

Normalizes the experience by providing prevalence data and emotional grounding. Reduces caregiver anxiety by showing that the situation is common and manageable.

Caregiver "About 1 in 3 children get ear infections before age 3. They're common, treatable, and won't cause hearing loss."
Child (6–12) "Lots of kids get earaches. It's not your fault and you'll feel better soon."

When to use: Any time a health event could trigger worry. Always include prevalence data when available. Based on PMC mHealth anxiety research showing that normalization reduces health anxiety by 40%.

Pattern 02

Next Step Block

Provides a single, actionable next step. Cuts through decision paralysis by reducing options to the most helpful action right now.

Caregiver "The most helpful thing right now: give 5 ml of ibuprofen and check temperature again in 30 minutes."
Child (6–12) "Here's one thing to try: drink some water and rest for a bit."

When to use: After any assessment, diagnosis, or health alert. Follow the AHRQ "need to know vs. nice to know" principle: 2–3 essential actions maximum. Never overwhelm with a full treatment plan.

Pattern 03

Safety Net Block

Provides clear escalation criteria without triggering alarm. Structured as a decision tree: "Call your doctor during office hours if [X]. Go to the ER if [Y]."

Non-emergency (neutral card) "Call your doctor during office hours if the fever lasts more than 3 days, or if your child seems unusually tired."
Emergency (bordered card) "Go to the ER if your child has difficulty breathing, a stiff neck, or a fever above 105°F."

Implementation: Non-emergency criteria use a neutral gray card. ER-level criteria use a bordered card (not red background — red backgrounds trigger panic). Uses AAP proportionate language framework: match messaging severity to clinical risk.

Pattern 04

Empowerment Block

Celebrates effort and progress without addictive reward loops. Reinforces agency and self-efficacy using growth mindset language.

Caregiver "You noticed this early — that's exactly what helps. Trust your instincts."
Child (6–12) "You tried something new today. That takes courage."

Key rule: Praise the process, not the outcome (Dweck). Never "You're the best" or competitive framing. Never streak counters. Never "You're on a roll!" — this creates anxiety about breaking the streak.

Pattern 05

Parent Gate Copy

Language for features behind caregiver verification. Must be clear, non-manipulative, and respect children's understanding.

Gate prompt "This section is for grown-ups. A parent or guardian can unlock it."
Challenge "To continue, solve this: What is 12 + 7?"

Implementation: Use a knowledge-based challenge (simple math, hold-to-unlock for 3 seconds), not just a button. Never use a CAPTCHA. Follows UK ICO Age Appropriate Design Code Standard 13: no nudge techniques that push children toward lower-privacy options. Source: ico.org.uk

Content System

Accessibility for Neurodiversity

ADHD and anxiety are the most common neurodevelopmental conditions in children. Our interfaces must work for how these brains actually process information — not how we wish they would.

Cognitive Load Reduction Rules

Based on W3C COGA guidelines and WCAG 2.2.

  1. Chunking: Max 5 choices per screen. Break multi-step flows into single-action screens with a progress indicator. More than 5 options triggers decision paralysis, especially in children with ADHD. (W3C COGA: Avoid too much content)
  2. Progressive disclosure. Show essential info first. Secondary details go behind a "Learn more" expand. Never front-load a wall of text.
  3. Predictable navigation. Same menu order on every screen (WCAG 3.2.3 Consistent Navigation). Breadcrumbs for all multi-level flows. Never rearrange navigation based on "personalization."
  4. Simplification support. Offer an option to hide non-essential features. Some users need a minimal interface. (W3C COGA: Support Simplification)
  5. No time pressure. Allow users to extend or eliminate time limits (WCAG 2.2.1). No countdown timers for form submission. No auto-advancing carousels.
  6. Authentication without memory burden. Offer biometric, SMS code, or email magic link — not password-only. Password requirements ("8 chars, 1 uppercase, 1 symbol") are hostile to users with working memory challenges. (W3C COGA authentication pattern)

User Preference Toggles

The Motion toggle in the header toolbar reduces all animation durations to 0ms and respects prefers-reduced-motion. High-readability settings (18px base font, line-height 1.9, letter-spacing 0.01em, spacing multiplier 1.3) are applied globally by default for maximum legibility.

ADHD-Specific Content Patterns

One Thing at a Time

One CTA per screen. Secondary actions are visually muted (lower contrast, smaller size, no fill). The primary action is unmistakable. Never present "Save" and "Save & Continue" and "Cancel" at equal visual weight.

Preview the Journey

Before starting any multi-step flow, show the step count and current position. "Step 2 of 4" reduces anxiety about how much remains. Use a progress bar, not just numbers. Never hide the endpoint.

Gentle Re-engagement

If the user leaves mid-flow, save state automatically. When they return, offer "Pick up where you left off" — never "You didn't finish!" or "Don't lose your progress!" Guilt-based re-engagement is hostile to ADHD users.

Sensory Calm

Reduce animation in focus mode. No autoplay audio or video. Use muted color palettes when concentration is needed. High-contrast, high-saturation interfaces are energizing but fatiguing for extended use.

Applied Research

Child-Centered Design Patterns for Pediatric Medical Devices

Learn more. Scroll less. — When a child or caregiver interacts with a medical device UI, the stakes are higher than any consumer app. These patterns ensure that own-device interfaces (companion apps, on-device screens, wearable displays) are safe, clear, and child-appropriate. Clarity over consumption.

Why This Matters

Medical device UIs serve three distinct users — each with different needs:

  • The child patient — may need to read status, respond to prompts, or confirm actions. Cognitive load must be minimal.
  • The caregiver — manages settings, reviews data, makes clinical decisions. Needs clear information hierarchy.
  • The healthcare professional — configures device, reviews logs, adjusts parameters. Needs efficiency without sacrificing clarity.

Four critical risks drive every pattern below:

  1. Use error leading to harm (wrong dose, missed alarm, incorrect reading)
  2. Cognitive overload causing the child to disengage from their own care
  3. Alarm fatigue leading to ignored critical alerts
  4. Unsupervised access to settings that require clinical judgment

References: FDA Human Factors Guidance [8], FDA Patient Labeling [9], EU MDR 2017/745 [10], IEC 62366-1, ISO 14971.

D.1

Critical Task Mapping

Problem

Device UIs often treat all screens with equal visual weight. A settings screen looks identical to a dose-confirmation screen. When a child or caregiver can’t instantly distinguish routine tasks from safety-critical ones, errors increase.

Solution

Assign every screen a criticality tier (Routine / Important / Critical) and apply consistent visual coding. Critical screens get maximum contrast, minimal distractions, and require explicit confirmation. Routine screens can be richer in content.

Implementation Steps

  1. Inventory all device screens and classify by criticality tier.
  2. Define visual language per tier: Critical = high-contrast, single action, confirmation required. Important = clear hierarchy, limited choices. Routine = standard UI patterns.
  3. Add a visible tier indicator (e.g., colored top bar or icon) so users build mental models over time.
  4. Test with children: can they identify which screens “really matter” within 3 seconds?

Do

Use a red top bar + large icon for critical screens (dose confirm, alarm ack).

Don’t

Use the same card layout for “View history” and “Confirm dose.”

D.2

Safe Defaults

Problem

Out-of-box or post-update settings may default to values that assume an adult user: small text, aggressive alarm thresholds, advanced data views. A child encountering these defaults may misinterpret information or miss critical alerts.

Solution

Ship every device with the safest reasonable default for a child user. Auto-advance OFF, alarms at maximum sensitivity, text at largest size, simplified view first. Adults can opt into complexity; children should never have to opt out of danger.

Implementation Steps

  1. Audit every configurable setting. For each, determine: “What is the safest value for a 6-year-old using this alone?”
  2. Set that as the factory default.
  3. Group advanced settings behind a Parent Gate (see D.8).
  4. On first setup, show a guided “personalization wizard” that adjusts defaults by age band.

Do

Default to largest text, simplest view, loudest alarms, auto-advance OFF.

Don’t

Default to “compact view” or “expert mode” that obscures critical information.

D.3

Clear State Visibility

Problem

Children (and stressed caregivers) need to know the device’s current state at a glance: Is it working? Is something wrong? Is it connected? Ambiguous status indicators (blinking LEDs, tiny icons, jargon labels) cause anxiety and errors.

Solution

Design a persistent status region visible on every screen. Use a traffic-light model: green = all good, yellow = needs attention, red = act now. Pair color with icon and text — never rely on color alone (accessibility). Make the status region the first thing the eye sees.

Implementation Steps

  1. Define all possible device states (connected, measuring, error, low battery, paused, calibrating, etc.).
  2. Map each to a traffic-light tier + icon + short label (≤3 words).
  3. Place the status region in a consistent position (top of screen, always visible).
  4. For each state, define what the child should do (“All good — keep playing” vs. “Show this to mom/dad”).
  5. Test: can a 6-year-old tell you if the device is “happy” or “needs help” in under 2 seconds?

Do

Show a large, friendly status icon + one-line text: “ Everything’s working!”

Don’t

Show “Status: 0x04E — BLE Connected” in 10px gray text.

D.4

Undo & Recover

Problem

Children explore by tapping. In a medical device UI, accidental actions (changing a setting, dismissing an alarm, skipping a step) can have real consequences. Without undo, every tap carries anxiety.

Solution

Make every non-critical action reversible within 10 seconds. For critical actions, require a two-step confirmation with a clear “Go back” option. Never punish exploration — the child should feel safe to tap and learn.

Implementation Steps

  1. Classify all actions: reversible (can undo) vs. committed (cannot undo once confirmed).
  2. For reversible actions: show a toast with “Undo” for 10 seconds.
  3. For committed actions: require two-step confirm (“Are you sure? This will [action].”).
  4. Always show a visible “Back” button — never trap the user in a flow.
  5. Log all undo events for HFE analysis (high undo rates signal confusing UI).

Do

Show “Setting changed. Undo?” toast for 10 seconds after any setting change.

Don’t

Apply changes instantly with no way to reverse them.

D.5

Progressive Disclosure

Problem

Medical devices often need to show a lot of information: readings, trends, settings, alerts, instructions. Showing everything at once overwhelms children and increases use errors for all users.

Solution

Show only what the user needs for this moment. Start with the essential reading or status. Offer a clear “See more” or “Details” path for those who want it. Match the disclosure depth to the user’s age band and role (child sees simple; caregiver sees detail).

Implementation Steps

  1. For each screen, identify the one thing the user needs to know right now.
  2. Make that the hero element: largest, highest contrast, center of attention.
  3. Group additional details into expandable regions or secondary screens.
  4. Label expansions clearly: “More details”, “History”, “What does this mean?”
  5. In caregiver/professional mode, auto-expand more detail by default.

Do

Child mode: “Your reading is 95 ” with a “What does this mean?” link.

Don’t

Show SpO2 graph, heart rate trend, battery %, BLE status, and 3 alert banners all at once.

D.6

Alarm Design

Problem

Alarm fatigue is one of the top patient safety concerns in pediatric care. When devices generate too many alarms, or alarms are indistinguishable from each other, users stop responding — including to genuine emergencies. For children, alarming sounds can also cause fear and anxiety.

Solution

Design a three-tier alarm taxonomy that is visually, audibly, and haptically distinct. Minimize false alarms through smart thresholds. Make every alarm actionable — tell the user what to do, not just what went wrong. For children, pair urgency with reassurance.

Implementation Steps

  1. Classify every alert into Info / Warning / Critical. If in doubt, tier up.
  2. Design distinct sounds for each tier (test with children: can they tell them apart?).
  3. Every alarm screen shows: What happened + What to do + Who to tell.
  4. Critical alarms mirror to caregiver device within 2 seconds.
  5. Track alarm frequency — if >3 Info alarms/hour, consolidate into a summary.
  6. Never use alarms for marketing, engagement, or non-safety purposes.

Do

“Your reading is low. Sit down, have a snack, and check again in 5 minutes. ”

Don’t

“ALERT: VALUE OUT OF RANGE. ERROR CODE 0x2F.”

D.7

Help & Safety Net

Problem

Children encounter confusing screens and don’t know what to do. Caregivers forget steps they learned during onboarding. Help content buried in menus or PDFs doesn’t get used. When users can’t get help in context, they guess — and guessing in a medical device is dangerous.

Solution

Provide contextual, in-screen help accessible with one tap from any screen. For children, use age-appropriate language and visuals. Include a persistent “Safety Net” shortcut that always shows: emergency contact, what the device does, and “how to get help.” Integrate IFU (Instructions for Use) content directly into the UI flow, not as a separate document.

Implementation Steps

  1. Add a “?” or “Help” button on every screen, positioned consistently.
  2. Help content is contextual: the help for the alarm screen explains alarms, not general device info.
  3. Include a “Safety Net” screen accessible from anywhere (floating button or persistent nav item):
    • Emergency contact number
    • “What does this device do?” in one sentence
    • “I need help right now” — calls caregiver or shows emergency steps
  4. IFU content chunked into the relevant screens, not a 40-page PDF.
  5. For caregivers: link to full IFU from the help screen.

Do

On the calibration screen: “Need help? Tap here to see how to calibrate step-by-step.”

Don’t

Link to a 40-page PDF IFU from a tiny “i” icon in the footer.

D.8

Parent Gate (On-Device)

Problem

Children may accidentally (or intentionally) access device settings that require clinical judgment: dosing parameters, alarm thresholds, connectivity settings, or data export. Unrestricted access creates real medical risk.

Solution

Gate all configuration and clinical settings behind a Parent Gate. Use the same pattern as The Child Lens Parent Gate component (math challenge + hold), adapted for the device form factor. The gate must be effective for children but not frustrating for caregivers under stress. Never use gates on emergency or safety features.

Implementation Steps

  1. Classify all settings: child-safe (can adjust freely) vs. gated (requires caregiver).
  2. Gate mechanism: age-appropriate challenge (math problem for 6+, hold-to-unlock for smaller screens) + biometric if available.
  3. Gate timeout: lock after 5 minutes of inactivity in settings.
  4. Never gate: emergency contacts, help, safety net, alarm acknowledgment.
  5. Log all gate bypass attempts for HFE analysis.

Do

Gate dose settings with a math challenge: “What is 7 × 4?” + hold for 2 seconds.

Don’t

Put a “Settings” icon in the nav bar with no protection.

Child-Centered Design Patterns for Pediatric Biotech

Learn more. Scroll less. — even off screen. Packaging, leaflets, clinical posters, consent forms, and sample kits are a child’s “UI” for biotech products. These patterns ensure that non-digital touchpoints are clear, calm, and designed for developing minds. Less screen. More sense.

Why This Matters

Every physical touchpoint — a medicine box, an instruction leaflet, a clinic poster — is a moment where a child or caregiver makes a decision. Confusing packaging leads to dosing errors. Dense leaflets get ignored. Unclear consent forms create anxiety instead of understanding.

The same principles that make digital experiences child-friendly apply here: one idea at a time, pictures before words, clear stopping points, and age-appropriate language (CDC Clear Communication Index [7]).

References: CDC Clear Communication Index [7], FDA Patient Labeling [9], EU MDR 2017/745 [10], Dickey et al. [11], Balkan et al. [12].

B.1

Packaging & Labeling

Problem

Pediatric biotech products often use adult-oriented packaging: small text, dense information hierarchy, regulatory-first layout. Caregivers under stress (middle of the night, sick child) can’t find the critical information: dosage, age range, preparation steps, expiration.

Solution

Design packaging with three visual layers: (1) Instant recognition — large product name, age range, key pictogram; (2) Quick reference — dosage, preparation summary, warnings; (3) Detail — full regulatory text, batch info, ingredients. Layer 1 is visible from arm’s length. Layer 2 is visible when holding the box. Layer 3 is readable with effort.

Implementation Steps

  1. Identify the 3 things a caregiver needs in the first 5 seconds: product name, age suitability, key action.
  2. Design Layer 1 at ≥18pt with ≥4.5:1 contrast ratio.
  3. Use color coding consistently across product line (e.g., age bands by color).
  4. Place preparation steps on the inside of the box lid or a pull-out card — not buried in a folded leaflet.
  5. Test with caregivers: can they find dosage info in <10 seconds in dim light?

Do

Large product name + age band color + single pictogram on front face.

Don’t

Fill the front face with regulatory text, barcodes, and 8pt ingredient lists.

B.2

IFU One-Pager

Problem

Instructions for Use (IFU) documents are often multi-page, dense, and written for regulators rather than users. Caregivers don’t read them. When they do, they can’t find the one piece of information they need right now. This is a patient safety issue.

Solution

Create a single-page visual IFU that covers the 5 essential actions: Prepare, Use, Monitor, Store, Dispose. The full regulatory IFU still exists, but this one-pager is the primary reference. Designed to be stuck on a refrigerator or pinned next to the bed. All steps shown as numbered pictograms with ≤10 words each.

Implementation Steps

  1. Identify the 5–7 essential steps (distill from the full IFU).
  2. Create a numbered pictogram for each step.
  3. Max 10 words per step. Use imperative voice (“Wash hands” not “Hands should be washed”).
  4. Include a QR code linking to video demonstration and full IFU.
  5. Print on water-resistant stock if it will be used near water/food.
  6. Test: can a caregiver follow the steps without reading the full IFU?

Do

5 numbered pictograms with short labels on a single laminated sheet.

Don’t

An 8-page folded insert in 6pt type with no pictures.

B.3

Child-Friendly Preparation Sheet

Problem

Many biotech products require preparation steps (reconstitution, mixing, measuring). Children who participate in their own care preparation feel more in control and less anxious — but only if the steps are presented at their level. Adult-only prep instructions exclude the child from their own health journey.

Solution

Create a side-by-side preparation sheet: one column for the caregiver (full detail), one column for the child (simplified, pictogram-based). The child column uses age-appropriate language and shows what they can do (“You hold the cup”, “You count to 10”). This builds agency while the caregiver maintains safety.

Implementation Steps

  1. Map all preparation steps. For each, determine: can the child participate safely?
  2. Child-safe steps get a child-column entry with a pictogram and simple instruction.
  3. Adult-only steps show in the caregiver column with a “Grown-up does this” icon in the child column.
  4. Use consistent color coding: child steps in a calming color, caregiver steps in neutral.
  5. Include a “All done!” celebration at the end (sticker, checkmark, high-five prompt).

Do

Two-column sheet: “Your job” (child) + “Grown-up’s job” (caregiver) with pictograms.

Don’t

A single dense paragraph: “Reconstitute the lyophilized powder by adding 5mL of diluent...”

B.4

Consent & Assent (Offline)

Problem

Informed consent forms are often written in legal/medical language that parents struggle with — and children can’t understand at all. Children over 7 have the right to “assent” (agree in an age-appropriate way) to medical procedures, but assent forms are often afterthoughts.

Solution

Design two documents: (1) A caregiver consent form written at SMOG grade 6, with key decisions highlighted and a clear “What this means” sidebar. (2) A child assent form tailored to age band, using pictures, simple language, and honest descriptions of what will happen (“This might feel cold” not “Minor discomfort may occur”).

Implementation Steps

  1. Start from the legally required content. Identify: what must the parent/child understand?
  2. Caregiver consent: rewrite in plain language (SMOG ≤ grade 6). Highlight decisions in yellow. Add “What this means:” plain-English sidebar for each section.
  3. Child assent: use age-appropriate format (see age notes). Be honest about discomfort. Give the child a genuine choice (“You can say no”).
  4. Both forms should end with a clear summary: “What happens next” in 3 bullet points.
  5. Test with actual families in the target age range.

Do

Child assent: “We want to check your blood. It uses a tiny poke on your finger. It might sting for a second. You can say no.”

Don’t

Child assent: “I consent to phlebotomy as described in Section 4.2 of the protocol.”

B.5

Teach-Back Card

Problem

Caregivers and children leave clinical encounters unsure of what to do at home. Verbal instructions are forgotten within hours. Written handouts are lost or never read. Without teach-back, there’s no way to verify understanding before the family leaves the clinic.

Solution

Design a physical teach-back card: a sturdy card (credit-card to postcard size) with 3–5 key actions on one side and a “teach it back” prompt on the other. The clinician reviews the card with the family, then asks the child (age-appropriate) or caregiver to explain it back. The card goes home as a reference. Comprehension is verified before discharge.

Implementation Steps

  1. Identify the 3–5 actions the family must do correctly at home.
  2. Front side: numbered pictogram steps (same style as IFU One-Pager).
  3. Back side: “Now you try!” prompts (“Tell me: what do you do first?”, “Show me: how do you hold it?”).
  4. Print on durable card stock; design to survive a school backpack.
  5. Include a sticker or stamp space for “I taught it back! ”

Do

Postcard-size, 4 pictogram steps front, “Now you try!” prompts back.

Don’t

Send the family home with a verbal “Any questions?” and a photocopied handout.

B.6

Clinic Signage & Wayfinding

Problem

Pediatric clinics are stressful for children. Confusing signs, clinical language, and adult-oriented wayfinding increase anxiety. A child who can’t find the bathroom, doesn’t know what “phlebotomy” means, or can’t tell how long the wait will be is a child whose stress response is activated — making clinical interactions harder.

Solution

Design clinic signage at two heights and two reading levels: adult signs at standard height with clinical terms + plain English, and child-level signs (≤3 ft) with pictograms, friendly names, and color-coded wayfinding. Every sign should answer: “Where am I?” and “What happens here?”

Implementation Steps

  1. Audit the patient journey from entrance to exit. Identify every decision point.
  2. At each point, place: adult sign (eye level, clinical + plain) and child sign (child eye level, pictogram + friendly name).
  3. Use consistent color coding per area (e.g., blue = waiting, green = play, yellow = exam rooms).
  4. Include wait-time indicators children can understand (“About 2 cartoons long”).
  5. Name rooms in child-friendly terms alongside clinical names (“The Check-Up Room” under “Examination Room 3”).

Do

Child-height sign: “The Check-Up Room — A doctor will look at how you’re growing!”

Don’t

A door labeled “Exam 3” in Arial 12pt at adult eye level.

B.7

Sample & Supply Handling

Problem

Children interact with medical supplies and samples: holding collection cups, wearing monitors, applying patches, managing consumables (test strips, cartridges). Poor labeling, confusing steps, and undifferentiated supplies lead to errors, contamination, or waste.

Solution

Design supply handling for mistake-proof use: color-code supplies by step order, use shape-coding to prevent mix-ups (different caps, different sizes), include “use by” indicators that children can understand (not just expiration dates), and provide clear disposal instructions with pictograms.

Implementation Steps

  1. Map the supply flow: open → prepare → use → store/dispose.
  2. Color-code supplies by step (e.g., blue cap = step 1, green cap = step 2).
  3. Use shape/size differentiation to prevent wrong-supply errors.
  4. Expiration: “Use before [date]” in large text + a traffic-light color sticker system (green = good, yellow = use soon, red = expired).
  5. Disposal: pictogram on each supply item showing correct disposal (trash, sharps, return).
  6. Include a visual “supply check” card: “Do you have everything?” with pictures of each item.

Do

Color-coded caps by step order + disposal pictogram on each item.

Don’t

Identical-looking supplies labeled only with tiny part numbers.

B.8

Physical Stop Cues

Problem

In non-digital contexts, “stopping points” are equally important. Children who don’t know when a procedure is done feel anxiety. Caregivers who don’t have a clear “finished” signal may repeat steps or skip the final verification. Medical procedures without clear endings leave everyone uncertain.

Solution

Design explicit stop cues into every physical material and procedure: a “Done!” checkmark at the end of instructions, a completion sticker or stamp, a verbal script for the clinician (“All done! You did great.”), and a “What happens next” card for take-home. Just as in digital: Designed to finish, not to linger.

Implementation Steps

  1. Every instruction sheet/card ends with a visible “ All done!” marker.
  2. Procedures include a final verification step: “Check: [3 things to confirm].”
  3. Provide a completion reward appropriate to age: sticker, stamp, certificate, verbal praise script.
  4. Include a “What happens next” card: when to come back, what to watch for, who to call.
  5. Train clinicians on the stop cue script: acknowledge, celebrate, explain next steps.

Do

“ All done! Here’s what happens next:” card + sticker.

Don’t

Instructions that trail off with “Repeat as needed” and no clear ending.
