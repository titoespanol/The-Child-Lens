Typography Detail

Alternate Glyphs

Lenia Sans ships with stylistic alternates — a second set of letterforms built into the font file. We activate them on headlines and display text to add a playful, child-friendly touch that feels warm and approachable.

Why alternate glyphs?

Children respond to friendly, rounded shapes. The alternate forms of a, r, g, and y in Lenia Sans have softer, more organic curves that feel less “formal” and more inviting. We use them in headings and splash screens to create a sense of fun — while keeping body text in standard forms for maximum readability.

Implementation

Typography Detail

Mixed-Weight Headings

Lenia Sans’s five weights (300–700) let us create visual rhythm inside a single heading. Mix bold and light to guide the eye, create emphasis, and add warmth—all while keeping legibility and hierarchy.

Weight Cascade

Progressively increase weight across phrases to build momentum.

Further. Faster. Forward.
span.fw-light   “Further.” span.fw-medium  “Faster.” span.fw-bold   “Forward.”

Bold + Light Contrast

Pair bold (700) with light (300) to create emphasis through contrast. The lighter word often uses an accent color.

Learn More. Scroll Less.
Child-first Design System
span.fw-bold          “Learn More.” span.fw-light.text-accent “Scroll Less.”

Emphasis via Weight + Color

Use medium (500) as the base, with bold (700) accent-colored keywords for emphasis.

Safety, Trust & Ethics
span.fw-medium         “Safety,” span.fw-bold.text-accent “Trust” span.fw-medium         “&” span.fw-bold.text-accent “Ethics”

Do

Limit to 2–3 weight levels per heading for clarity

Do

Combine with alt-glyphs—add the .alt-glyph span inside the weight span

Don’t

Don’t mix weights in h3–h6 or body copy—reserve for h1 and h2 only

Don’t

Don’t make the lighter portion longer than the bold portion—bold should carry the primary meaning

Implementation

Wrap heading words in <span> with .fw-light, .fw-bold, etc. Add .text-accent for accent color. Alt-glyph spans nest inside weight spans: <span class="fw-bold">Le<span class="alt-glyph">a</span>rn More.</span>

Stories

Why stories? Stories are a familiar, low-pressure format for children. Unlike clinical text, stories use narrative, metaphor, and gentle guidance to explore mental health topics. Each story is fully scrollable and child-paced: no auto-advance, no timers, no pressure. Children read at their own speed and close when they’re ready. Designed to finish, not to binge.

Tap a circle to open the story — scroll to read at your own pace

Interaction Model

Tap any story circle to open it. Scroll vertically to read at your own pace. Press the “Done Reading” button or the × close button when finished. Keyboard: Escape closes the viewer. Focus is trapped inside the story for accessibility. No auto-advance, no timers.

Do

Let children scroll at their own pace — each story has substantial, richly formatted content

Do

End each story with a hands-on activity and a clear “Done Reading” CTA

Don’t

Auto-advance stories or use timers — children need control over their reading pace

Don’t

Use short, shallow content — stories should be substantive enough to teach, not just entertain

Age adaptation: Content reading level adapts by age group. Ages 0–2: picture-focused with minimal text. Ages 3–5: simple sentences with visual cues. Ages 6–8: full paragraphs with activities. Ages 9+: research-informed content with reflection prompts. All ages: no auto-advance, child controls their own pace.

Comprehension Check

Why this exists: Research shows active recall improves retention by 50% compared to passive re-reading [3][4]. By asking one question after reading, we shift the goal from “time spent” to “understanding gained.” This is the first stage of the Learn Better Loop: Comprehend. Apply. Stop.

Try it — select an answer

What does “Learn More. Scroll Less.” mean for children’s apps?

The Research

The testing effect: retrieving information strengthens memory more than re-reading. Delgado et al. [3] found screen reading yields ~13–15% lower comprehension than paper. Active recall structures close this gap. Quality minutes, not screen minutes.

Do

Ask 1 question per content unit. Keep it simple.

Do

Show calm feedback — no penalties, no scores

Don’t

Use timed quizzes — pressure harms comprehension

Don’t

Gate progress on correct answers — learning is the goal, not scoring

Age adaptation: Ages 3–5: picture-based answers. Ages 6–9: short text options. Ages 10+: full text. Caregiver mode shows correct answer rate.

One-Sentence Recap

Why this exists: Summarization forces synthesis — the highest-order thinking skill. When a child restates a concept in their own words, they’re not just remembering — they’re understanding. This is stage 2 of the Learn Better Loop. Comprehend. Apply. Stop.

Try it — write a recap

Tell it in one sentence:

The Research

Summarization requires the reader to identify the main idea, discard irrelevant details, and restate in their own words. This is cognitively demanding — and that’s the point. Less screen. More sense.

Do

Accept any honest attempt — celebrate effort

Do

Age-adapt the prompt: “Explain it to mom/dad in 1 sentence”

Don’t

Grade or score the recap — it’s for reflection, not testing

Don’t

Require a minimum word count

Age adaptation: Ages 3–5: voice input or emoji selection. Ages 6–9: short text input with prompt. Ages 10+: full sentence. Caregiver mode shows recap history.

Apply & Practice

Why this exists: Transfer — applying knowledge in a new context — is the bridge between screen and real world. A 60-second hands-on action solidifies learning more than 10 minutes of passive scrolling. This is stage 2 of the Learn Better Loop. Comprehend. Apply. Stop.

Do

Save & Stop

Why this exists: Designed to finish, not to binge. The “Done for today” button is more prominent than “Continue” because stopping is the healthy default. WHO [1] and AAP [2] both recommend limiting screen time. This component makes that limit a first-class design element — not a setting buried in parental controls.

Notice which button is primary

The Philosophy

Designed to finish, not to binge. Sessions end with celebration, not cliffhangers. “Done for today” is success, not failure. A child who stops after 3 focused minutes did better than one scrolling for 30.

Do

Make “Done for today” the largest button

Do

Show time summary in caregiver mode

Don’t

Add “Just one more?” prompts

Don’t

Penalize stopping with lost streaks or sad characters

Age adaptation: All ages see “Done for today” as primary CTA. Caregiver mode shows session time summary + recap.

Feelings

Understanding Feelings

A guide to your emotional world

Your Feelings Are Real and Important

Every single day, you experience many different feelings. Sometimes you feel happy and full of energy, like when you're playing your favorite game. Other times, you might feel sad, angry, scared, or confused. All of these feelings are completely normal. There is no such thing as a "bad" feeling — every emotion is your brain's way of telling you something important about the world around you.

Research in child psychology shows that children who learn to name and understand their emotions develop stronger coping skills, build better relationships, and perform better in school. When you know what you're feeling, you can make thoughtful choices about what to do next instead of just reacting.

The Feelings Thermometer

Think of your feelings like a thermometer. At the bottom, where it's cool, you feel calm and relaxed. In the middle, you might feel a bit excited or nervous — like before a birthday party or a test. Near the top, your feelings are very strong — maybe you're really angry, extremely scared, or incredibly excited. None of these levels are wrong. The thermometer just helps you notice how strong your feeling is right now.

When your thermometer is near the top, your body gives you clues: your heart beats faster, your muscles get tight, your stomach might feel funny, or your face might get hot. Learning to notice these body signals is the first step toward managing big emotions.

Tips for Understanding Your Feelings

  • Name it to tame it — say "I feel angry" or "I feel worried" out loud. Naming a feeling makes it less overwhelming.
  • Check your body — tight shoulders, fast breathing, or a stomachache are clues about what you're feeling.
  • Remember that feelings come and go like waves — even the strongest ones pass with time.
  • Talk to a trusted adult when your feelings feel too big to handle alone. Asking for help is brave, not weak.
  • Draw, write, or act out your feelings if words are hard to find. Creative expression is a powerful way to process emotions.

Try It Now: The Feelings Check-In

Close your eyes for a moment. Take one slow breath in and one slow breath out. Now ask yourself: "What am I feeling right now?" Try to find a name for it. Maybe it's "curious" because you're learning something new, or "relaxed" because you're reading. There's no wrong answer. Just notice what's there and say, "Hello, [feeling]. I see you."

Calm

Calm & Breathe

Your body's built-in superpower

Breathing Is Your Secret Superpower

Did you know that you have a superpower you can use anytime, anywhere? It's something you already do thousands of times a day without even thinking about it: breathing. But here's the secret — when you breathe slowly and on purpose, something amazing happens in your body. Your heart slows down, your muscles relax, and your brain gets a signal that says, "Everything is okay. You're safe."

Scientists who study the brain have discovered that deep, slow breathing activates your parasympathetic nervous system — that's the part of your body's control center responsible for calming you down. When you feel stressed, scared, or overwhelmed, your body goes into "alert mode." Intentional breathing is like pressing a gentle reset button that brings you back to a calm, clear-thinking state.

Three Breathing Techniques Just for Kids

Star Breathing: Hold up one hand with your fingers spread like a star. With the pointer finger of your other hand, trace up the outside of your thumb while breathing in slowly. Trace down the other side while breathing out. Continue for each finger. By the time you finish all five fingers, you'll have taken five deep, calming breaths.

Balloon Belly: Place both hands on your belly. Imagine there's a balloon inside your tummy. Breathe in slowly through your nose for 4 counts and feel the balloon inflate — your hands should rise. Then breathe out through your mouth for 6 counts and feel the balloon gently deflate. Repeat three times.

Hot Chocolate Breathing: Cup your hands like you're holding a warm mug of hot chocolate. Breathe in through your nose to "smell" the chocolatey goodness (4 counts). Then blow gently out through your mouth to "cool it down" (6 counts). This technique works especially well because it gives your mind a comforting image to focus on.

Tips for Building a Calm-Down Routine

  • Practice breathing when you're already calm so it becomes automatic when you're upset.
  • Pick one technique that feels best to you and make it "yours" — your go-to calm-down tool.
  • Create a calm-down corner at home with soft pillows, a favorite toy, and a card reminding you of your breathing steps.
  • Use breathing before tests, performances, or doctor visits to help your body feel ready instead of afraid.
  • Teach your breathing technique to a friend or family member — teaching others helps you remember it even better.

Try It Now: Balloon Belly

Put down whatever else you're holding. Place both hands flat on your belly. Close your eyes if that feels comfortable. Now breathe in through your nose for 1… 2… 3… 4 — feel your belly push your hands outward. Hold for just a moment. Now breathe out through your mouth for 1… 2… 3… 4… 5… 6 — feel your belly sink back down. Do this two more times. Notice how your body feels after those three breaths. A little calmer? That's your superpower at work.

Worries

Worry Monsters

Shrinking your worries down to size

What Are Worry Monsters?

Imagine your worries as little monsters that live inside your mind. When everything is calm, they're tiny — about the size of a marble. But when something stressful happens — a big test, a fight with a friend, moving to a new school — those worry monsters start to grow. If you ignore them, they can get so big that they take up your whole brain and make it hard to think about anything else.

Here's what's important to know: worry is actually your brain trying to protect you. It's like an alarm system. The problem is that sometimes the alarm goes off when there's no real danger — like worrying that everyone at school is laughing at you when really nobody noticed your mismatched socks. Psychologists call these "false alarms," and they happen to everyone, especially kids whose brains are still growing and learning what's truly dangerous and what's not.

How Worry Tricks Your Brain

Worry monsters are sneaky. They use tricks to make things seem scarier than they really are. One trick is called "catastrophizing" — that's when your brain jumps straight to the worst possible outcome. For example: "If I get one answer wrong on the test, I'll fail the whole class, and everyone will think I'm stupid." In reality, one wrong answer on one test doesn't mean any of that.

Another trick is "mind reading" — when your worry monster tells you that you know exactly what other people are thinking: "They're definitely laughing at me." But the truth is, you can't actually read minds. Most of the time, other people are too busy thinking about their own stuff to notice the things you're worried about. Recognizing these tricks is half the battle.

Tips for Shrinking Your Worry Monsters

  • Give your worry monster a silly name and a funny appearance. It's harder to be scared of "Blobbert the blue worry blob."
  • Ask yourself: "Is this a real problem happening right now, or is my brain guessing about the future?" Most worries are about things that haven't happened yet.
  • Use the "best friend test" — if your best friend told you this worry, what would you say to them? Now say that kind thing to yourself.
  • Set a "worry time" — give yourself 10 minutes a day to think about worries, then close the worry box and move on.
  • Tell a trusted adult about your worry monsters. Big feelings get smaller when you share them out loud.

Try It Now: Draw Your Worry Monster

Grab a piece of paper and some colored pencils or crayons. Think about one thing that's been worrying you lately. Now draw that worry as a silly monster. Give it a goofy face, weird arms, and a funny hat. Write its name underneath. Then draw yourself next to it — much bigger and stronger. You're the boss of your worry monster, not the other way around. Keep the drawing somewhere you can see it to remind yourself that worries are manageable.

Friends

Being a Good Friend

Building connections that matter

Why Friendships Matter for Your Health

Friendships are one of the most important parts of growing up — and they're actually good for your health. Research shows that children who have at least one close friendship have lower levels of stress, feel happier, and are more confident. Friendships teach you skills you'll use your whole life: how to share, how to listen, how to solve problems together, and how to be kind even when it's hard.

But friendships can also be tricky. Sometimes friends disagree. Sometimes someone says something hurtful. Sometimes you feel left out. These challenges are painful, but they're also normal. Every friendship, even the best ones, has difficult moments. What matters most isn't avoiding conflict — it's learning how to handle it with kindness and honesty.

The Three Pillars of Friendship

Kindness: Being a good friend starts with treating others the way you'd want to be treated. This doesn't mean always agreeing — it means being respectful even when you disagree. Kindness includes small things: saving a seat, sharing a snack, noticing when someone looks sad and asking if they're okay.

Listening: One of the greatest gifts you can give a friend is your full attention. Real listening means putting down your phone, looking at your friend, and actually hearing what they say — not just waiting for your turn to talk. When a friend tells you something important, you can show you listened by saying it back: "It sounds like you felt left out at recess. That must have been really hard."

Boundaries: Good friends also respect boundaries. If your friend says "I don't want to play that game," a good response is "Okay, what would you like to do instead?" — not pressuring them. And you have the right to set boundaries too. Saying "no" to something you're uncomfortable with doesn't make you a bad friend; it makes you honest.

Tips for Being a Great Friend

  • Celebrate your friends' successes genuinely — being happy for others strengthens your bond and builds trust.
  • Apologize when you make a mistake. A real apology sounds like "I'm sorry I did that. I'll try to do better." — not "Sorry you felt that way."
  • Include others who seem lonely. Being the person who says "Come sit with us" can change someone's entire day or even their year.
  • Stand up for your friends when they're being treated unfairly, even when it's scary. Courage is being afraid but doing the right thing anyway.
  • Remember that being a good friend to yourself matters too. You deserve the same kindness you give to others.

Try It Now: The Friendship Letter

Think of one friend who has been important to you — someone who made you laugh, helped you through a hard time, or just makes your days brighter. Now write them a short note or draw them a picture. It can be as simple as: "I'm glad you're my friend because…" and fill in why. You can give it to them tomorrow or keep it for yourself. Either way, thinking about what you appreciate in your friendships helps your brain focus on the good — and that's great for your mental health.
